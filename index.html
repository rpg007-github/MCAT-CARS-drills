What is CARS?
Understanding the game before you play it

üìñ The CARS Section at a Glance

The Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills section tests your ability to comprehend, analyze, and reason about dense passages from the humanities and social sciences. You get 9 passages and 53 questions in 90 minutes ‚Äî roughly 10 minutes per passage. There is no outside knowledge required. Every answer is in or supported by the passage.

Passages come from fields like philosophy, history, literature, art criticism, sociology, and ethics. They are often dense, opinion-driven, and deliberately complex.

The Golden Rule

The CARS section does not test what you know. It tests how well you read and reason. A passage about 18th-century Portuguese pottery is not testing your knowledge of pottery ‚Äî it is testing whether you can understand an argument and reason about it. Everything you need is on the page.

üß† The Four Reasoning Foundations

AAMC categorizes CARS questions into four foundations of reasoning:

  • Foundations of Comprehension (~30%): What did the author literally say? Main idea, vocabulary in context, factual recall from the passage.
  • Reasoning Within the Text (~30%): How does the argument work? Identifying evidence, author's purpose, how one paragraph supports another.
  • Reasoning Beyond the Text (~40%): What would the author agree with? How would new information affect the argument? These questions extend beyond the passage itself.

‚è±Ô∏è The Time Challenge

At roughly 10 minutes per passage, you have about 3‚Äì4 minutes to read and 1 minute per question. Most students fail CARS not because they don't understand the passage, but because they spend too long reading and not enough time reasoning on questions.

The goal of these drills is to build automatic reading instincts so that comprehension requires less active effort, freeing your cognitive bandwidth for reasoning and answer evaluation.

Proven Reading Strategies
Techniques that actually work on test day
Core Principle

Your goal while reading is not to understand every detail. Your goal is to understand the author's main point, their attitude toward it, and the structure of their argument. Details can be found later when a question asks about them.

1 Find the "Why"

Every CARS passage exists to make a point. As you read, ask: "Why is the author telling me this? What do they want me to believe?" The answer is usually in the first or last paragraph.

2 Track Attitude Shifts

Watch for words like "however," "but," "yet," "although," and "despite." These signal the author's real position and where they push back against other views.

3 Active Paragraph Summaries

After each paragraph, mentally note its function in one phrase: "gives example," "introduces counterargument," "author's main claim," "historical context." This builds a passage map.

4 Don't Pre-Solve

Read to understand, not to predict questions. Students who try to "figure out what they'll ask" while reading often miss the author's actual argument. Stay in the text.

5 The Two-Pass Method

On questions, read all four answers before committing. The "first good answer" is often a trap. The best answer must be both true according to the passage AND responsive to what the question actually asks.

6 Scope Control

Wrong answers are often too broad, too narrow, or outside the passage's scope. Ask: "Is this what the passage is actually about, or did I bring outside knowledge here?"

üîë The Author's Attitude Is Everything

Roughly 40‚Äì50% of CARS questions ask you to reason about the author's perspective in some way ‚Äî main argument, attitude, likely agreement with a new claim. Build the habit of identifying:

  • What does the author believe? (Their central claim)
  • What do they oppose? (What they argue against)
  • How confident are they? (Hedging language vs. certainty)
  • What is their tone? (Critical, celebratory, cautious, nostalgic, etc.)

If you can answer these four questions after reading a passage, you can handle most CARS questions without re-reading large sections.

The Four Question Types
Know what each question type is really asking

üí° Main Idea / Central Thesis

Foundation: Comprehension

These ask: "What is this passage primarily about?" or "Which best summarizes the author's central argument?"

The trap: Answers that are true but too narrow (just about one paragraph) or too broad (beyond what the passage actually argues).

The fix: The correct answer must account for the entire passage ‚Äî why the author started AND ended where they did.

üîç Tone & Attitude

Foundation: Comprehension

These ask: "What is the author's attitude toward X?" or "How does the author view this perspective?"

The trap: Picking an extreme tone when the author is measured, or vice versa. Also, confusing the author's own view with a position they are describing but not endorsing.

The fix: Find language that reveals emotion or judgment. Words like "unfortunately," "merely," "rightly," or "naively" are attitude markers.

üèóÔ∏è Argument Structure

Foundation: Reasoning Within Text

These ask: "What is the function of this paragraph?" or "How does the example in paragraph 3 support the author's argument?"

The trap: Describing what the paragraph says (its content) rather than what it does (its function).

The fix: Think in terms like: "This provides evidence for...", "This introduces a counterargument that the author then refutes...", "This qualifies the previous claim by..."

üîÆ Inference & Application

Foundation: Reasoning Beyond Text

These ask: "Which of the following would the author most likely agree with?" or "How would the author respond to this new claim?"

The trap: Picking answers that sound good or seem logical but aren't grounded in what the author actually argued. Outside reasoning kills here.

The fix: Tie your answer back to a specific sentence or idea from the passage. If you can't point to where the passage supports it, be very cautious.

Common Trap Answers
The mistakes that separate 125s from 132s

üéØ The "Too Extreme" Answer

Authors of CARS passages almost always use measured, qualified language ("tends to," "may suggest," "often"). Trap answers use extreme language like "always," "never," "proves," or "completely eliminates."

FIX: If an answer uses extreme language, verify the author actually used that level of certainty. Usually they didn't.

üåä The "True But Wrong" Answer

This is the most common wrong answer on CARS. The statement is true according to the passage ‚Äî it's mentioned somewhere ‚Äî but it doesn't answer the specific question asked. You feel good about it because it's "in there."

FIX: Read the question stem carefully. Ask: "Is this answer actually addressing what was asked, not just something true about the passage?"

üìö The "Outside Knowledge" Trap

You bring in facts you know from biology, history, or other fields that the author never mentions. The passage is about art theory and you select an answer based on neuroscience you remember from other studying.

FIX: CARS is a closed universe. If it's not in the passage, it doesn't exist for these questions. Be suspicious of any knowledge you're adding from outside.

üîÑ The "Author vs. Other View" Confusion

CARS authors frequently describe viewpoints they disagree with before arguing against them. Students select answers that match the opposing view the author is critiquing, thinking it's the author's position.

FIX: Track who is speaking at every moment. Flag phrases like "some argue," "critics claim," "others believe" ‚Äî these are NOT the author's position.

üé™ The "Sounds Like The Right Topic" Answer

This answer is about the right subject matter but gets the relationship wrong. The passage argues that art reflects economic conditions; the trap says art shapes economic conditions. Same topic, reversed logic.

FIX: Don't just match topic words ‚Äî verify the direction and nature of any claimed relationship matches the passage.

üìè The "Too Specific / Too General" Mismatch

For main idea questions especially, one wrong answer will summarize only one section of the passage. Another will make a claim far broader than the author actually makes.

FIX: The correct main idea answer must cover the whole passage without going beyond it. Test every candidate answer against the opening and closing paragraphs.
